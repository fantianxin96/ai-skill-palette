Turn messy product/design input into clear, actionable product insight.

## When To Use

Use this when the user provides user feedback, interview notes, competitor observations, app reviews, chat logs, social comments, stakeholder opinions, or scattered ideas and wants to understand the real product/design opportunities.

Do not jump straight to solutions. First synthesize what the evidence actually says.

## Workflow

1. **Clarify the source**
   - Identify what kind of material is being synthesized: user feedback, interview notes, competitor notes, analytics, personal observations, or mixed material.
   - Separate direct evidence from assumptions and the user's own interpretation.

2. **Cluster themes**
   - Group repeated needs, frustrations, desires, and behavior patterns.
   - Name each theme in user-centered language.
   - Mark evidence strength: strong repeated signal, weak signal, single anecdote, or assumption.

3. **Extract insight**
   - For each theme, explain the underlying user problem, emotional driver, and product implication.
   - Distinguish symptoms from root causes.

4. **Create opportunities**
   - Convert insights into opportunity statements.
   - Suggest possible UX/product directions without overcommitting to one solution.
   - Note which opportunities are quick wins, strategic bets, or research gaps.

5. **Prioritize**
   - Rank by user impact, confidence, effort, and fit with the product goal.
   - Recommend what to design, prototype, or validate next.

## Output Format

- Source summary
- Themes and evidence
- Key insights
- Opportunity areas
- Recommended product/design actions
- Open questions or research gaps

## Quality Bar

A good synthesis should make the next product decision clearer. It should reduce noise, reveal patterns, and help the user understand why a feature or page should exist before designing it.

Never pretend weak evidence is strong. Label uncertainty plainly.
